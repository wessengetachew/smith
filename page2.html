The Gap Diagonal Identity

Classical result (direct consequence of Euler's totient function): for the lattice $\{(r,M) : 1 \le r \le M \le G\}$, the fraction of pairs on the diagonal $|M-r|=g$ that are coprime equals $\varphi(g)/g$.

What's new here: the geometric framing — reading the lattice as a family of diagonal strips, each carrying its own density, as a way to decompose and visualize C(n). The identity itself is classical; the decomposition perspective is this project's contribution.

Proof Sketch
$$\gcd(r, r+g) = \gcd(r, g)$$
Step 01 · Parametrize the diagonal▶
Points on diagonal $|M-r|=g$ are exactly the pairs $(r, r+g)$ for $r=1,\ldots,G-g$. The coprimality condition is $\gcd(r, r+g)=1$.
Step 02 · Reduce the coprimality condition▶
By the key identity $\gcd(r, r+g) = \gcd(r, g)$, coprimality with $r+g$ reduces to coprimality with $g$ alone.
Step 03 · Count via periodicity▶
The condition $\gcd(r,g)=1$ is periodic in $r$ with period $g$. Among any $g$ consecutive integers, exactly $\varphi(g)$ are coprime to $g$, giving asymptotic density $\varphi(g)/g$.
Step 04 · Exactness and error bound▶
The density $\varphi(g)/g$ is exact when $G-g$ is a multiple of $g$; otherwise the error is bounded by $\varphi(g)/(G-g)$, vanishing as $G\to\infty$. The formula is thus asymptotically exact for all fixed $g$ as $G$ grows.
Extension to Block-Coprimality — The C(n) Setting

At $n=0$, diagonal strip $g$ has coprime density exactly $\varphi(g)/g$ — a classical fact. As $n$ grows, the block-coprime condition imposes additional constraints: a pair $(r, r+g)$ must now satisfy $\gcd(r, r+g+j) = 1$ for $j = 0, \ldots, n$. This modifies the local density on each strip by eliminating extra residue classes at each prime dividing $g, g+1, \ldots, g+n$.

The formula below gives the block-coprime density restricted to diagonal strip $g$ at depth $n$:

$$\prod_{p \mid g(g+1)\cdots(g+n)} \!\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right)$$

Note: this per-strip formula is a heuristic for how each diagonal behaves individually. The global C(n) is not exactly a weighted average of these per-strip densities — that would require the strips to be independent, which they are not (they share prime factors). The diagonal decomposition is a visualization tool and a useful approximation, not an exact identity at n>0.

Why This Matters
  • Geometric decomposition (this project): reading the lattice as diagonal strips, each with its own density φ(g)/g, gives a visual explanation for why C(n)'s Euler product looks the way it does — each prime p contributes most strongly through the diagonals it divides.
  • Classical: the density φ(g)/g on diagonal g is a direct consequence of Euler's totient formula and periodicity — no new mathematics, but a useful geometric restatement.
  • Connection to Hardy–Littlewood (speculative, not established here): the per-strip block-coprime density is structurally similar to the local factors in Hardy–Littlewood's singular series for prime constellations. This is a suggestive parallel worth noting, but this page does not establish any formal connection — the Hardy–Littlewood conjecture remains unproven and the relationship would require careful additional work to make precise.

Invisible Forests — Dense Non-Coprime Patches

Herzog–Stewart 1971: for any n×m, there exists a block of entirely non-coprime cells. These appear as large dark rectangular patches at high G. First 2×2 invisible forest: (2,2),(2,4),(4,2),(4,4). Connected to CRT and consecutive composites.

Density Field — Local Coprime Density
$$D_R(r, M) = \frac{1}{(2R+1)^2} \sum_{|r'-r|\le R,\, |M'-M|\le R} \mathbf{1}[\gcd(r',M')=1]$$

The gradient $\nabla D_R$ points away from the main diagonal $r=M$ (the coprime-sparse seam where $\gcd(r,r)=r>1$) toward regions of higher coprime density, especially the $g=1$ strips where every adjacent pair is coprime. High-magnitude gradient arrows cluster around the edges of invisible forests (Herzog–Stewart 1971), encoding a local version of the Mertens error term.
